How dynamic media delivery works

One master is stored. Every derivative is generated on demand and cached at the edge — no renditions are created, stored or managed. The ownership boundary is not a gap in the architecture; it is the control point.

Who delivers it is a choice, not a lock-in

The delivery layer is a competitive market of mature, interchangeable providers. Because Screendragon holds the origin and mints the URLs, the provider underneath can be selected on merit and changed later without re-platforming the asset library. This is what composable actually means in practice.

What should decide it
  • How much video ASDA's library actually carries. This is the single biggest differentiator between these providers, and it rules imgix in or out.
  • Whether the multi-CDN requirement is a live operational need or an inherited one. True multi-CDN adds a routing layer, cost and complexity, and the incumbent does not provide it today either.
  • ASDA's actual delivery volume, which is on their current invoice. Pricing at this scale is negotiated, not listed.
  • Whether ASDA would rather retain a provider they already hold a contract with. The architecture supports that without change.
  • Buy or build. A managed SaaS partner runs the delivery layer for a margin; the AWS-native route removes that margin but we deploy and operate it. The right answer depends on ASDA's AWS footprint and how much operational load Screendragon should carry.

The report the client sees is ours — and it's a differentiator

"Which URLs get the most hits, which are live, which are dead" is a Screendragon output. It is assembled by joining two data sources, and only one of them belongs to the delivery layer. The value is in the join — which is why a standalone CDN cannot produce this report.

The views that fall out of the join — none of which delivery traffic alone can show
  • High traffic, near expiry. Renew these before they lapse.
  • Still being served, but should be dead. Rights lapsed while the edge kept serving from cache — a live compliance exposure.
  • Published, but zero traffic. Dead weight and decay.
One precision point. "Dead" has several distinct causes — an expired URL signature, an asset unpublished in the DAM, rights that have lapsed, or simply no traffic. Different states, different fixes, and the reliable liveness signal comes from the gate, not the edge. Also worth knowing: on AWS, per-URL traffic comes from exporting and aggregating CloudFront logs rather than a single packaged analytics call, so that reporting pipeline is a little more to build than with imgix. The differentiating join, though, is ours to build either way.

How this gets billed

Delivery is a variable cost that scales with traffic. The software is not. Keeping those two apart in the commercial model is the single most important decision in this part of the bid.

Recommended

Model A — ASDA contracts delivery directly

  • ASDA holds the delivery contract and pays the provider directly
  • Screendragon charges a fixed fee for integration, the publish pipeline and signed-URL minting
  • Screendragon carries no egress cost and no public uptime liability
  • ASDA negotiates delivery on their own volume — a better rate, with no reseller margin on top
  • Delivery can be swapped later without touching Screendragon
Trade-offTwo suppliers, two invoices.
Viable, with conditions

Model B — Screendragon primes, partner disclosed

  • One contract, one invoice, with the delivery partner named as a disclosed subcontractor under RFP clause 2.3
  • Delivery is an itemised pass-through line — not bundled, not fixed
  • A stated volume assumption, with a defined mechanism for what happens above it
  • Screendragon takes a management fee for the wrapper, not the delivery risk
Trade-offOnly works with a watertight volume and overage clause. Without one, this quietly becomes Model C.
Do not do this

Model C — all-inclusive fixed bundle

  • Delivery absorbed into a single fixed, all-inclusive price
  • A six-figure variable bill sitting underneath a fixed fee
  • Overage is charged at a premium and unused capacity is forfeited — the cost only moves one way
  • Payment terms are 120 days, so the bill is fronted for four months
  • One bad peak-trading forecast eats the margin on the whole deal
